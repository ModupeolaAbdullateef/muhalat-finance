How compound interest works

Compound interest means you earn returns not just on your original investment, but also on the interest you've already accumulated. Over time, this "interest on interest" effect becomes increasingly powerful — this is why starting early and staying consistent is the greatest investment advantage you have.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a realistic rate of return in Nigeria?
Nigerian equities have historically returned 10–18% annually on the NGX. Money market funds average 15–20%, while treasury bills and bonds vary with CBN policy. A conservative estimate of 10–12% is common for long-term planning, though inflation should also be factored in.
Should I invest a lump sum or monthly contributions?
Both are powerful strategies. A lump-sum investment gives your money more time to compound, while regular monthly contributions (called naira-cost averaging) reduce the risk of investing at the wrong time. The best approach is often both — invest what you have now and continue adding monthly.
How much should I invest per month?
A common guideline is to invest at least 15% of your gross income for retirement. If you're just starting, even ₦10,000–₦20,000 per month makes a meaningful difference over time. The key is consistency — start with what you can and increase as your income grows.
What investment options are available in Nigeria?
Popular options include NGX-listed stocks, mutual funds, money market funds, treasury bills, FGN bonds, real estate investment trusts (REITs), and pension fund accounts (RSA) through PFAs. Diversifying across asset classes helps balance risk and reward.
Does inflation affect my investment returns?
Yes. With Nigeria's inflation rate often in double digits, your real (inflation-adjusted) return is lower than the nominal figure. For example, a 15% return with 18% inflation gives a negative real return. Aim for investments that consistently outpace inflation over time.
How does compound frequency affect growth?
The more frequently interest compounds, the more you earn. Monthly compounding produces slightly more than annual compounding over long periods. For example, ₦1,000,000 at 12% for 20 years compounds to ₦9.85M monthly vs. ₦9.65M annually — a meaningful difference.
